Hadeeth on Jealousy (Hasad)

This lesson helps us understand the hadeeth about jealousy, see how it appears in real life, and learn simple ways to clean the heart from hasad.

The Hadeeth

إِيَّاكُمْ وَالْحَسَدَ، فَإِنَّ الْحَسَدَ يَأْكُلُ الْحَسَنَاتِ كَمَا تَأْكُلُ النَّارُ الْحَطَبَ
Iyyākum wal‑ḥasad, fa‑inna al‑ḥasada yaʾkulu al‑ḥasanāt kamā taʾkulu an‑nāru al‑ḥaṭab.
“Beware of jealousy, for jealousy eats up good deeds just as fire eats up wood.”
Reported in Sunan Abī Dāwūd and mentioned with similar wording in other collections.

Read the hadeeth slowly and imagine what happens to good deeds when jealousy stays in the heart.

Picture Reflection: Fire and Wood

The picture shows how fire quickly eats the wood. In the same way, jealousy can slowly eat the reward of our good actions if we choose not to remove it.

Understanding Jealousy

What is hasad?

Hasad means wanting a blessing to be removed from another person, as if we do not want them to enjoy what Allah has given.

It is different from admiring a good quality and asking Allah to give us something similar without wishing any harm for them.

Why is it dangerous?

The hadeeth reminds us that jealousy can destroy the reward of good deeds, similar to how fire burns and finishes dry wood.

Hasad also damages relationships, makes the heart hard, and steals inner peace from the one who feels it.

Root causes in the heart

  • Weak trust that Allah gives everyone their share with perfect wisdom and justice.
  • Ingratitude and forgetting to count our own blessings and favors from Allah.
  • Constant comparison with others in looks, marks, wealth, or popularity.
  • Low self-worth, feeling that another person’s success reduces our own value.
  • Too much love for dunya, where status and praise become more important than the pleasure of Allah.

Hasad in daily life

Jealousy can begin with a small thought when we see someone praised, gifted, or successful, and we feel tightness in the chest.

If it is not treated, this feeling can turn into bad words, cold behaviour, or even actions that try to harm the other person.

Training the heart

  • Say “Allāhumma bārik” and make du‘ā for the person when you see a blessing that you like.
  • Make a habit of daily shukr by writing down three things you are grateful for each day.
  • Remember that Allah’s treasures never finish, so another person’s blessing does not block your rizq.
  • Ask Allah often: “O Allah, purify my heart from hasad and fill it with contentment.”

Training the mind

  • Limit unhealthy scrolling and comparison on social media.
  • Look at effort, good character, and obedience to Allah more than outside appearance.
  • Turn jealousy into motivation: do more good deeds instead of wishing for someone to lose theirs.
  • Remind yourself that real honor comes from being close to Allah, not from people’s attention.

Story of Prophet Yusuf عليه السلام

The picture reminds us of how the jealousy of Prophet Yusuf’s brothers pushed them toward a very wrong decision, even though Yusuf عليه السلام was innocent.

The story in brief

Prophet Yusuf عليه السلام was loved deeply by his father, Prophet Ya‘qub عليه السلام. His brothers became jealous and allowed envy to grow in their hearts.

That jealousy led them to plot against Yusuf, throw him into a well, and lie to their father. Even with their harm, Allah protected Yusuf and later raised his status in a beautiful way.

Key lessons

  • Jealousy can push a person from a feeling in the heart to serious actions like lying, betrayal, and cruelty.
  • Yusuf عليه السلام chose patience and trust in Allah instead of hatred, and Allah honoured him in the end.
  • When the brothers later regretted, Yusuf forgave them, showing that mercy heals what jealousy once damaged.

Hasad in the Modern World

Where we see it today

  • On social media, when people compare followers, holidays, gifts, or results and feel upset inside.
  • In school, when someone is unhappy that a classmate got higher marks or was chosen for a special role.
  • In friendships, when a person wants attention only for themselves and dislikes when others are praised.
  • In families, when brothers or sisters are compared and the heart becomes bitter instead of loving.

How to respond

  • Remember that online life shows only the best moments, not the full story of people’s struggles.
  • Reduce time spent scrolling and increase time spent on Qur’an, du‘ā, and useful skills.
  • When you notice hasad starting, pause and say: “Allāhumma bārik lahum wa‘ṭinā min faḍlik.”
  • Replace comparison with gratitude and ask, “What is one good deed I can do right now instead?”
