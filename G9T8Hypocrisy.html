Hadith Lesson — Sahih al-Bukhari & Muslim

آيَةُ الْمُنَافِقِ — The Signs of the Hypocrite

A foundational hadith on character and moral integrity in Islam

آيَةُ الْمُنَافِقِ ثَلَاثٌ إِذَا حَدَّثَ كَذَبَ وَإِذَا وَعَدَ أَخْلَفَ وَإِذَا اؤْتُمِنَ خَانَ

"The signs of a Munaafiq (hypocrite) are three: If he speaks, he lies; if he makes a promise, he breaks it; and if he is given a trust, he betrays it."

⭐ Narrated by Abū Hurayrah (رضي الله عنه)  |  Collected by al-Bukhārī & Muslim
📚 Narrator:  Abū Hurayrah
📖 Sources:  Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī & Muslim
🔢 Signs:  3 (+ 1 in another narration)
🏫 Topic:  Akhlāq / Character
📖

General Meaning of This Hadith

What is a Munaafiq (منافق)?

In English, a Munaafiq is a hypocrite — literally, one who pretends to be what he is not. In the Islamic context, the true Munaafiq is a disbeliever who outwardly pretends to be Muslim. Allāh (سبحانه وتعالى) has described their severe punishment:

Quranic Evidence

إِنَّ الْمُنَافِقِينَ فِي الدَّرْكِ الْأَسْفَلِ مِنَ النَّارِ وَلَن تَجِدَ لَهُمْ نَصِيرًا

"Verily, the hypocrites will be in the lowest depths of the Hell-fire, and you will find no helper for them."

— Sūrah an-Nisāʾ (4):145

This hadith also warns that any believer who adopts one of these characteristics has taken on a trait of hypocrisy — not making them a full hypocrite, but placing them in a dangerous state known as nifāq ʿamalī (practical hypocrisy).

🔢 The Fourth Sign

In another narration from ʿAbdullāh ibn ʿAmr, a fourth sign is mentioned:

وَإِذَا خَاصَمَ فَجَرَ

"If he argues/disputes, he becomes immoral (foul-mouthed, transgresses boundaries)."

The Prophet ﷺ also warned in another hadith: "A man's cursing of his parents is among the major sins." This happens indirectly when someone curses another's parents, provoking a chain reaction of insults.

⚖️

Key Distinction: Two Types of Nifāq

Islamic scholars make an important distinction. Having a trait of a hypocrite does not make one a hypocrite in creed. There are two categories:

🔴 Nifāq Qalbī

Hypocrisy of the Heart

  • Outwardly professes Islam
  • Inwardly disbelieves
  • The true Munaafiq
  • Will be in the lowest depths of Hell
  • Combines ALL traits permanently
🟢 Nifāq ʿAmalī

Hypocrisy in Actions

  • A believing Muslim
  • Commits acts of hypocrites
  • Still has faith (imān) in heart
  • In serious danger — must repent
  • Occasional or habitual bad traits
Scholarly Note: The Prophet ﷺ mentioned these traits as a warning to believers — not to label them as full hypocrites, but to alert them that consistently practising these traits can gradually lead a person toward true disbelief. The more a person combines these traits habitually, the closer they drift to nifāq qalbī.

📊 Comparison: Nifāq Qalbī vs. Nifāq ʿAmalī

Aspect Nifāq Qalbī Nifāq ʿAmalī
Belief in heart❌ Disbelieves✅ Believes
RulingKāfirMuslim with sin
HereafterLowest HellRisk of punishment; can repent
TraitsAll, permanentlySome, habitually
SolutionSincere acceptance of IslamRepentance and self-reform

The Four Signs — Detailed Analysis

1

إِذَا حَدَّثَ كَذَبَ

🗣️ If He Speaks, He Lies

Lying takes many forms beyond outright fabrication. It includes exaggeration to impress, half-truths that mislead, and joking lies that amuse an audience. The Prophet ﷺ warned:

"Woe be to the one who speaks and lies to make people laugh. Woe be to him. Woe be to him."

The habit of exaggerating can silently turn a person into a habitual liar. Lying harms the liar — the Prophet ﷺ said: "Verily lying leads to immorality (fujūr), and immorality leads to the Hell-fire." (Ibn Masʿūd)

⚠️ Permitted Exceptions (Hadith of Asmāʾ bint Yazīd):
  • A husband saying kind, pleasing words to his wife (and vice versa)
  • Lying during wartime (military strategy, not treachery)
  • Lying to reconcile two disputing Muslims
2

إِذَا وَعَدَ أَخْلَفَ

🤝 If He Makes a Promise, He Breaks It

This refers to someone who consistently and deliberately breaks their commitments — someone known among the people as unreliable. Allāh commands the believers to honour their covenants:

Quranic Reference

يَـٰٓأَيُّهَا الَّذِينَ ءَامَنُوٓا۟ أَوْفُوا۟ بِالْعُقُودِ

"O you who believe, fulfil your contracts." — Sūrah al-Māʾidah (5):1

The true believer goes to great lengths, even at personal sacrifice, to keep their word. The Qurʾān praises those:

Quranic Reference

وَالْمُوفُونَ بِعَهْدِهِمْ إِذَا عَاهَدُوا

"Those who fulfil their promises when they make them." — Sūrah al-Baqarah (2):177

⚠️ This does NOT apply when: the person is genuinely unable due to unforeseen circumstances, the promised act was later found to be forbidden, or it was a case of forgetfulness rather than intentional deception.
3

إِذَا اؤْتُمِنَ خَانَ

🔒 If Given a Trust, He Betrays It

The Arabic word amānah (trust/trust-worthy) is broad — it includes money, secrets, responsibilities, and even the trust of relationships. Man often treats others' property less carefully than his own. Allāh's wisdom in recommending written contracts:

Quranic Reference

وَلَا تَسۡـَٔمُوٓا۟ أَن تَكۡتُبُوهُ صَغِيرًا أَوۡ كَبِيرًا إِلَىٰٓ أَجَلِهِۦ

"Do not be averse to writing down the contract, whether of little or great value, up to the time of its payment." — Sūrah al-Baqarah (2):282

Betrayal of trust extends beyond property — it includes betraying secrets shared in confidence, abusing authority, and neglecting duties entrusted to a person.

4

وَإِذَا خَاصَمَ فَجَرَ

🗡️ If He Argues, He Becomes Immoral

When engaged in dispute, this person abandons all ethical limits — resorting to slander, abusive language, attacking opponents' families, and transgressing sacred boundaries. This is the most visible sign in social interactions.

"Verbally abusing a Muslim is immorality (fusūq), and fighting him is kufr (disbelief in its action)." — The Prophet ﷺ

The Prophet ﷺ explained the mechanism: one person curses another's father or mother — the other responds in kind — creating a chain of mutual dishonour. The Muslim must argue with evidence and calm reasoning, never with insults.

⚠️ From another narration: Whoever combines ALL four of these traits is described by the Prophet ﷺ as a pure hypocrite (munāfiq khāliṣ).

Application in Daily Life

These four signs manifest in everyday situations. Recognising them in ourselves is the first step toward reform.

🏠

Family Life

Promising a family member to be home at a certain time, then repeatedly breaking it without valid reason — this is a sign of nifāq.

Family
🤝

Business Dealings

Accepting payment for goods or services, then delivering something inferior or nothing at all — a betrayal of amānah in commerce.

Workplace
💬

Social Media

Spreading unverified news, exaggerating stories for likes, or sharing content you know to be false — this is lying in digital form.

Digital Life
🏛️

Public Leadership

Making election promises with no intention to fulfil — a clear sign of promise-breaking on a societal scale.

Society
🤫

Keeping Secrets

A friend confides in you, and you share their secret — this is a betrayal of the trust (amānah) of confidentiality.

Relationships
😡

Online Arguments

When debating online, resorting to insults, attacking someone's character, and using foul language — the digital version of "fajar" (arguing immorally).

Digital Life

Student Life — Real Scenarios

How do these signs appear in school and student life? Examine each scenario and reflect:

📝

Sign 1: Lying — Exams & Homework

Copying a classmate's homework and claiming it is your own original work — or telling the teacher the assignment was not given.
Submitting your own work honestly; if you struggled, say so to the teacher and seek help.
📅

Sign 2: Breaking Promises — Study Groups

Agreeing to join a study group session, but repeatedly cancelling at the last minute without valid reason, leaving your friends waiting.
Only commit to what you can fulfil. If circumstances change, inform others early and apologise sincerely.
💼

Sign 3: Betrayal of Trust — Class Duties

Being elected class monitor (ketua kelas) but neglecting responsibilities — not recording attendance, losing class property, or misusing your position.
Fulfil every duty of your position to the best of your ability. A role is an amānah — a sacred trust.
💢

Sign 4: Immoral Arguments — Peer Conflicts

When disagreeing with a classmate, using insults, humiliating them publicly, mocking their family background, or bullying online.
Disagree calmly with evidence. Say: "I think differently because..." Never attack the person — address the issue.
💻

Modern Life — The Digital Age

The Prophet ﷺ described these signs 1,400 years ago — yet they are more relevant than ever in the age of social media, instant messaging, and digital commerce.

📱 Social Media Lying

  • Spreading fake news (berita palsu) for engagement
  • Misrepresenting yourself in online profiles
  • Clickbait titles that exaggerate the truth
  • Deep-fakes and manipulated images
  • Plagiarism of content without attribution

🛒 E-Commerce & Digital Trust

  • Online sellers misrepresenting products
  • Scam transactions (penipuan online)
  • Not delivering after receiving payment
  • Fake reviews to deceive buyers
  • Data privacy breaches — misusing others' information

🌐 Online Discourse

  • Cyberbullying and online harassment
  • Toxic comment sections with personal attacks
  • Cancel culture through false accusations
  • Doxxing (exposing private information)
  • Threatening language in political debates

✅ The Muslim's Digital Code

  • Verify before sharing (tabayyun) — Qurʾān 49:6
  • Maintain your online persona = your real character
  • Keep digital commitments as seriously as verbal ones
  • Protect others' digital privacy (amānah)
  • Respond to disagreements with adab (etiquette)
Think Before You Post: The Prophet ﷺ said: "Whoever believes in Allāh and the Last Day, let him say good, or remain silent." (al-Bukhārī & Muslim) — This applies equally to typing as to speaking. Every post, comment, and message is a record.
⭐

Key Takeaways

  • 🎯

    Purpose of the Hadith: A warning from the Prophet ﷺ to believers — these four traits are hallmarks of the munaafiqoon. Consistently practising them moves one toward true nifāq.

  • 📊

    Two Types of Nifāq: Nifāq Qalbī (disbelief at heart = kāfir) vs. Nifāq ʿAmalī (sinful believer who acts hypocritically but still believes). The distinction is critical in Islamic jurisprudence.

  • 🗣️

    Lying's Danger: Lies — even small, "harmless" exaggerations — open the door to greater sins. Truthfulness must be actively cultivated with patience: "The truth is bitter."

  • 🤝

    A Muslim is His Word: Your commitment and reliability define your Islamic character. Only promise what you can fulfil, and fulfil what you promise.

  • 🔒

    Amānah is Broad: Trust covers money, secrets, responsibilities, authority, and relationships. Protect every trust placed in you as you would guard your most precious possession.

  • 💬

    Ethical Disagreement: Disagreement is natural and even necessary — but the method must remain dignified. Attack the argument, never the person.

  • 💻

    Digital Accountability: Online behaviour is not exempt from Islamic ethics. Every digital interaction is witnessed by Allāh (سبحانه وتعالى) and recorded.
