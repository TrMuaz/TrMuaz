Section 1

The Story of Kaladah ibn Hanbal

The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ was a living example of good manners. One day, a Companion named Kaladah ibn Hanbal went to visit the Prophet without greeting or asking for permission to enter.

📖 The Story

Kaladah ibn Hanbal was sent by Safwan ibn Umayyah to bring gifts — milk, a young gazelle, and cucumbers — to the Messenger of Allah ﷺ who was in the upper part of Makkah at the time.

Kaladah entered the Prophet's house without greeting with Salam and without asking permission. The Prophet ﷺ immediately noticed this and corrected him with wisdom and gentleness.

The Prophet ﷺ said to him: "Go back and say: 'As-salamu ʿalaykum, may I come in?'"

Kaladah went back, greeted properly, asked for permission, and was then allowed to enter. He had received a lesson that stayed with him — and that we still learn today.

Hadith Reference
اِرْجِعْ فَقُلِ: السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُمْ، أَأَدْخُلُ؟

"Go back and say: 'As-salamu ʿalaykum (Peace be upon you), may I come in?'"

Sunan Abi Dawud 5133 · Sunan al-Tirmidhi 2710
💡
Key Lesson

Even sending gifts does not give you the right to enter without permission! The Prophet ﷺ corrected Kaladah not with anger, but with a simple instruction. This shows how gentle and educational the Prophet ﷺ was.

Section 2

Wisdom Behind the Hadith

Why did Islam place such importance on asking permission? There are deep wisdoms behind this teaching.

📖
Quranic Foundation — Surah An-Nur 24:27

Allah says: "O you who believe! Do not enter houses other than your own until you have asked permission and greeted their inhabitants. That is best for you, so that perhaps you will be reminded."

يَا أَيُّهَا الَّذِينَ آمَنُوا لَا تَدْخُلُوا بُيُوتًا غَيْرَ بُيُوتِكُمْ حَتَّىٰ تَسْتَأْنِسُوا وَتُسَلِّمُوا عَلَىٰ أَهْلِهَا
🛡️

Protecting Privacy

The Prophet ﷺ said: "Seeking permission was only made obligatory because of the eyesight" — so people are not seen in private moments. (Bukhari)

🕊️

Respecting Dignity

Everyone has the right to feel safe in their home. Entering without permission violates this sacred space and the dignity of its residents.

🤝

Building Trust

When we ask permission, we show that we respect the other person's choice. This builds stronger, more honest relationships.

✨

Spreading Salam

Asking permission is combined with Salam — a greeting of peace. This spreads love and goodwill, which are traits Allah loves for Muslims.

🧠

Teaching Self-Control

Not barging in requires patience and self-discipline — qualities that help us in all areas of life and that Islam constantly encourages.

🏡

Home as a Sanctuary

Islam treats the home as a private sanctuary — a place of rest and safety. Asking permission preserves this sanctity for every Muslim household.

Section 3

Manners When Visiting Someone's House

The Prophet ﷺ demonstrated a complete, beautiful system of etiquette when visiting others. Every step has a purpose.

  1. 1
    Stand to the Side of the Door

    Do not stand directly in front of the door. Stand to the right or left side. The Prophet ﷺ used to avoid facing the door directly, to protect the privacy of those inside from accidentally being seen.

  2. 2
    Knock Gently — Not Aggressively

    The Companions used to knock on the Prophet's door with the tips of their nails — softly. You are a guest, not a raider. Knock three times at moderate volume, with time between each knock to allow people to respond.

  3. 3
    Greet with Salam and Ask Permission

    Say the full greeting together with your request to enter:

    السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُمْ، أَأَدْخُلُ؟ "As-salamu ʿalaykum, a-adkhul?" (Peace be upon you, may I enter?)
  4. 4
    Identify Yourself When Asked

    If someone asks "Who is there?", give your full name clearly. Do not say "It's me" — the Prophet ﷺ strongly disliked this vague response, as it gives no useful information to the person inside.

  5. 5
    Wait Patiently Between Knocks

    Leave enough time between attempts — the person inside may be praying, eating, or in a state of rest. Some scholars say the gap should be long enough to complete four rakaʿat of prayer.

  6. 6
    Do Not Peek Inside

    The Prophet ﷺ said: "It is not permissible for a Muslim to look inside any house until he has been given permission." If you peer through windows or doors, you have already violated their privacy.

  7. 7
    If Refused — Leave Gracefully

    The Prophet ﷺ said: "If you seek permission three times and are not given it, then go back." This is not an insult — the residents may be busy, tired, or simply not ready for visitors. Accept this with good character.

ℹ️
Even Family Members

A man once asked the Prophet ﷺ: "Should I seek permission to enter my mother's room?" The Prophet said: "Yes." Even within the same household, privacy must be respected — especially during times of rest.

Section 4

Emergencies — When the Approach Differs

While asking permission is normally obligatory, Islam is a practical religion. There are specific circumstances where entering without the usual waiting process is allowed or even required.

If there is a fire, flood, or someone is in immediate mortal danger inside the house, you may enter without waiting for the usual three-knock permission process. Preserving life (hifz al-nafs) is one of the five essential objectives of Islamic Law (Maqasid al-Shariah). Scholars agree: entering to save a life or property is permitted when asking and waiting could lead to loss of life or property.

If someone inside is seriously injured, unconscious, or having a medical crisis and cannot respond, waiting for permission would be harmful. You should call out loudly first — "Is anyone home? There is an emergency!" — and if no response, act to save the situation. Always explain yourself clearly to avoid panic or misunderstanding.

If the person who can give permission has passed away, is travelling far away, or is otherwise unable to be reached — and something urgent must be done that cannot wait — scholars permit entering in this case. This covers situations such as caring for dependents left behind or attending to an urgent matter.

Entering shops, mosques, schools, or other non-residential public buildings does not require permission because general permission has already been given by the nature of those places being open to the public. However, back rooms, staff areas, or private offices within those buildings still require permission.

  • Shop floor — enter freely
  • Staff room behind the counter — ask first
  • Teacher's office — knock and ask
⚠️
Important Reminder

Emergencies are exceptions — not excuses. Do not stretch the definition of "emergency" to justify entering without permission whenever it is convenient. Islam values both saving lives and protecting privacy, and a true emergency must be genuine and urgent.

Section 5

Entering an Empty House

What if you know the house is empty — do you still need to say anything? Yes! The Quran and Sunnah give us guidance even for this situation.

📖
Surah An-Nur 24:28

"And if you find no one therein, still do not enter until permission has been given to you. And if you are asked to go back, go back — it is purer for you."

Even for an empty house, the following should be said upon entering:

What to Say When Entering an Empty House
السَّلَامُ عَلَيْنَا وَعَلَى عِبَادِ اللهِ الصَّالِحِينَ
As-salamu ʿalayna wa ʿala ʿibadillahis-saliheen
"Peace be upon us and upon the righteous servants of Allah."
Narrated by Abdullah ibn Umar · Collected in Adab al-Mufrad (1055) · Graded Hasan

Al-Albani explained: This practice fulfils the Quranic command to "greet one another with peace when you enter houses" (An-Nur 24:61). The house is not truly "empty" — there may be angels present, and this greeting acknowledges that we are guests even in our own space under Allah's watchful eye.

Additionally, saying Bismillah when entering any home (empty or not) protects the house from Shaytan, as the Prophet ﷺ taught: "If a man enters his home and says 'Bismillah', Shaytan says: 'You have no place to stay tonight and no dinner.'" (Muslim)

💡
For Your Own Home

When entering your own home, say Bismillah and greet with Salam even if no one seems to be there. This is Sunnah and keeps Shaytan away. If family is home, greet them warmly — it brings blessings to the whole household.

Section 6 · Surah An-Nur 24:58

Asking Permission Three Times

📖
Surah An-Nur 24:58

"O you who have believed, let those whom your right hands possess and those who have not yet reached puberty among you ask permission of you at three times: before the dawn prayer (Fajr), and when you put aside your clothing at noon, and after the night prayer (ʿIsha). These are three times of privacy for you..."

The rule of asking permission three times comes from the Prophet ﷺ himself:

Hadith — Bukhari 6245

"If one of you seeks permission to enter three times but is not given permission, let him go back."

Narrated by al-Bukhari (6245) and Muslim

The three special times of privacy mentioned in verse 24:58 apply especially within the household (to servants and children who have not yet reached puberty):

# Time Arabic Name Why Private?
1st Before Fajr (Dawn prayer) قَبْلَ صَلَاةِ الْفَجْرِ People may still be asleep or dressing for the early prayer
2nd Midday rest (Qaylula) حِينَ تَضَعُونَ ثِيَابَكُمْ مِنَ الظَّهِيرَةِ People remove extra clothing and rest; couples may be together
3rd After ʿIsha (Night prayer) مِنْ بَعْدِ صَلَاةِ الْعِشَاءِ Bedtime — people are changing into sleep clothes and resting
ℹ️
What "Three Times" of Knocking Means

There are two separate "three" rules: (1) Knock or ring the bell a maximum of three times each visit before leaving if no answer. (2) The three specific times of day when even household members must ask before entering private rooms. Both are based on the same principle: privacy is a right Islam protects at all times.

Section 7

Modern Manners: Texting & Messaging

The same Islamic principles that apply to visiting a home also apply to our digital lives today. Our phones and messages are an extension of our personal space.

Scholars note: "The same goes for texting. If you send someone a text and they did not respond, follow up — that is expressive of care and concern." But this must be done with wisdom and respect.

Just as you say "As-salamu ʿalaykum" before entering a house, start every text conversation with Salam. It is Sunnah, it creates goodwill, and it signals that your message comes with good intentions. Never jump straight into demands or questions without this greeting.

Sending message after message when someone doesn't reply immediately is like banging on someone's door repeatedly. Scholars draw a direct parallel: knocking three times = following up three times maximum. The person may be praying, studying, eating, or simply resting. Give them the benefit of the doubt.

Just as Surah An-Nur 24:58 mentions three private times of day, be mindful of when you message people. Avoid sending urgent messages late at night, at Fajr time, or during known prayer times. If it is not an emergency, it can wait. This shows you care about the other person's worship and rest.

Just as you should not peek into someone's house, do not read others' private messages without permission, share screenshots of private conversations, or access someone else's phone or accounts without consent. The hadith principle of "permission because of the eyesight" applies equally to digital spaces — what people share privately is their private space.

Just as the Prophet ﷺ disliked someone saying "It's me" without identifying themselves, don't assume the person knows your number. If you are messaging someone who may not have your number saved, introduce yourself: "Assalamu ʿalaykum, this is [your name] from class."

📌 Key Takeaways

  • Kaladah ibn Hanbal was corrected by the Prophet ﷺ for entering without greeting or asking permission — the proper way is "As-salamu ʿalaykum, may I enter?"
  • Asking permission protects privacy, preserves dignity, and spreads Salam — all core values in Islam.
  • Stand to the side, knock gently, identify yourself, and wait patiently — these are the manners of visiting.
  • Exceptions exist for genuine emergencies where life or property is at serious risk.
  • When entering an empty house, say: "As-salamu ʿalayna wa ʿala ʿibadillahis-saliheen."
  • The rule of three knocks (Bukhari 6245): if no answer after three attempts, leave. Surah An-Nur 24:58 identifies three private times of day within the household.
  • Islamic digital etiquette = same principles online: start with Salam, be patient, respect privacy, and give others the benefit of the doubt.
