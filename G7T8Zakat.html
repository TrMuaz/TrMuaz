Islamic Studies — Grade 9

Zakat: The Third Pillar of Islam

A comprehensive study of obligatory almsgiving — covering its definition, conditions, types, nisab thresholds, calculation methods, recipients, and spiritual significance. (Zakat Fitrah excluded)

60–90 mins Lesson Note 11 Sections
Learning Objectives
  • Define Zakat and distinguish it from Zakat al-Fitr and voluntary charity (sadaqah)
  • State the six conditions that make Zakat obligatory
  • Identify and explain the main types of Zakat (gold/silver, savings, trade, crops, livestock)
  • Calculate Nisab thresholds and determine the correct Zakat rate for each asset type
  • Name and describe the eight categories of Zakat recipients (asnaf)
  • Articulate the spiritual, social, and economic significance of Zakat
01

Definition of Zakat

Linguistic Definition
The word Zakat (الزكاة) comes from the Arabic root zakā, which carries two primary meanings: purification (tazkiyah) — the act of cleansing one's wealth and soul — and growth (numuw) — the idea that giving wealth causes it to increase through divine blessing.
Islamic/Technical Definition
Technically, Zakat is a fixed portion of specific categories of wealth, obligatory upon every free, sane, adult Muslim who possesses wealth equal to or above the minimum threshold (nisab) for at least one full lunar year (hawl), which must be distributed to designated categories of recipients as defined by the Quran and Sunnah.

Zakat is the Third Pillar of Islam, frequently mentioned in the Quran alongside Salah (prayer) — appearing together more than 80 times. It is not merely charity but a religious obligation (fard ayn) with a precisely defined scope, separate from voluntary giving.

Zakat must be distinguished from two related concepts: Zakat al-Fitr (a separate obligatory charity given at the end of Ramadan, excluded from this lesson) and Sadaqah (voluntary charity at any amount, given at any time). Zakat operates within strict rules of wealth type, minimum amount, time period, and eligible recipients.

02

Islamic Ruling & Evidence

Zakat is obligatory (fard) — its denial is an act of disbelief (kufr) according to the majority of scholars. It was made obligatory in the second year after the Hijrah (approximately 624 CE), at which point its detailed rules were revealed.

وَأَقِيمُوا الصَّلَاةَ وَآتُوا الزَّكَاةَ وَارْكَعُوا مَعَ الرَّاكِعِينَ
"And establish prayer and give Zakat and bow with those who bow [in worship and obedience]."
Surah Al-Baqarah (2:43)
خُذْ مِنْ أَمْوَالِهِمْ صَدَقَةً تُطَهِّرُهُمْ وَتُزَكِّيهِم بِهَا
"Take from their wealth a charity by which you purify them and cause them increase."
Surah At-Tawbah (9:103)
"Islam is built upon five [pillars]: Testimony that there is no god but Allah and that Muhammad is the Messenger of Allah; establishing of the prayer; giving Zakat; the pilgrimage to the House; and fasting in Ramadan."
Sahih al-Bukhari, No. 8 & Sahih Muslim, No. 16
"Whoever is made wealthy by Allah and does not pay Zakat on his wealth, then on the Day of Resurrection his wealth will be made like a bald-headed poisonous male snake with two black spots over the eyes. The snake will encircle his neck and bite his cheeks saying: 'I am your wealth, I am your treasure.'"
Sahih al-Bukhari, No. 1403
03

Conditions of Zakat

Zakat becomes obligatory only when all six conditions are simultaneously fulfilled. If any one condition is absent, Zakat is not due on that wealth.

  • 1
    Islam — Muslim Ownership
    الإسلام
    The owner of the wealth must be a Muslim. Zakat is not obligatory upon non-Muslims and is not accepted from them as an act of worship.
  • 2
    Full & Complete Ownership (Milkun Tām)
    الملك التام
    The wealth must be in the complete ownership and control of the individual. Wealth that is owed to others, loaned out, or under a trust does not qualify. The owner must have the right to use it freely.
  • 3
    Productive/Growth Potential (Namā')
    النماء
    The wealth must have potential for growth — either actual growth (like trade goods, livestock, crops) or potential growth (like gold, silver, and savings which can be invested). Personal items for daily use (home, car, clothing) are exempt.
  • 4
    Reaching the Nisab (Minimum Threshold)
    بلوغ النصاب
    The wealth must reach the minimum taxable threshold (nisab). Different categories of wealth have different nisab amounts. If wealth falls below nisab at any point during the year, the hawl (year) resets.
  • 5
    One Full Lunar Year Passes (Hawl)
    حولان الحول
    For most categories, the zakatable wealth must remain above nisab for one complete lunar year (approximately 354 days). Exception: crops and minerals are zakatable upon harvest/extraction, not after a year.
  • 6
    Surplus Above Basic Needs (Fāḍil ʿan al-Ḥājah)
    فاضل عن الحاجة الأصلية
    The wealth must exceed the basic needs of the individual and their dependents. Essential items such as the primary home, tools of trade, and personal transportation are excluded from the zakatable base.
04

Types of Zakat (Other than Zakat Fitrah)

The Quran and Sunnah identify five primary categories of wealth subject to Zakat. Classical scholars unanimously agreed on these; contemporary scholars have extended Zakat to include income and business assets by analogy (qiyās).

I
Zakat on Gold & Silver
زكاة الذهب والفضة
Includes
All gold and silver in any form — coins, jewellery, bars, whether worn or stored. Cash and savings are treated under gold/silver standards.
Nisab (Gold)
85 grams of pure gold (equivalent to 20 mithqal/dinars)
Nisab (Silver)
595 grams of silver (equivalent to 200 dirhams)
Hawl
One full lunar year must pass with wealth at or above nisab
Zakat Rate
2.5% of total value
Example
Gold weight: 100g × current price × 2.5% = Zakat due
II
Zakat on Savings & Cash
زكاة النقود والمدخرات
Includes
Bank savings, fixed deposits, cash at hand, foreign currency, investment accounts
Nisab
Equivalent value of 85g of gold OR 595g of silver (whichever standard applied)
Hawl
One full lunar year at or above nisab
Zakat Rate
2.5% of total savings
Deductions
Outstanding debts due within the year may be deducted before calculating
Note
Scholars recommend using the silver nisab as it is lower, benefiting more poor
III
Zakat on Trade Goods (ʿUrūḍ al-Tijārah)
زكاة عروض التجارة
Includes
All merchandise, inventory, stock, real estate held for resale, and business assets intended for trade
Nisab
Total market value of zakatable assets reaches equivalent of 85g gold or 595g silver
Hawl
One full lunar year from when business began or last Zakat anniversary
Zakat Rate
2.5% of net zakatable assets (cash + receivables + inventory at market value − current debts)
Fixed Assets
Machinery, vehicles, and property used in production (not held for resale) are exempt
Income Zakat
Contemporary extension: salaries/professional income reaching nisab annually → 2.5% (by qiyās)
IV
Zakat on Agricultural Produce
زكاة الزروع والثمار
Eligible Crops
Staple grains (wheat, barley, rice, corn) and storable fruits (dates, raisins) that humans cultivate as food
Nisab
5 wasqs ≈ 653 kg of dry grain or fruit (after threshing/drying)
Hawl
No hawl required — Zakat is due at every harvest
Rate (Rain-fed)
10% (ʿUshr) — Crops irrigated by rain, rivers, or natural flooding
Rate (Irrigated)
5% (Niṣf ʿUshr) — Crops requiring artificial irrigation (canals, pumps, wells)
Paid From
Zakat is given from the produce itself, not its monetary value (majority view)
V
Zakat on Livestock
زكاة الأنعام
Eligible Animals
Camels, cattle (cows/buffalo), and sheep/goats that graze freely on natural pasture (sāʾimah) for most of the year
Nisab (Camels)
5 camels — Zakat: 1 sheep for every 5 camels (up to 25); scales up with herd size
Nisab (Cattle)
30 cows — Zakat: 1 tabi' (one-year-old calf) for every 30; 1 musinnah (two-year-old) for every 40
Nisab (Sheep/Goats)
40 sheep/goats — Zakat: 1 sheep for 40–120; 2 sheep for 121–200; 3 sheep for 201–300; +1 for each additional 100
Hawl
One full lunar year for the herd to remain at or above nisab
Exemption
Animals used for labour (ploughing) or trade (to be sold) fall under trade goods rules, not livestock Zakat
VI
Rikāz — Buried Treasure & Minerals
زكاة الركاز والمعادن
Rikāz (Buried Treasure)
Pre-Islamic treasures or hoards discovered in the ground — Zakat due immediately upon discovery
Rikāz Rate
20% (Khums) — one-fifth, regardless of amount or nisab
Minerals (Maʿdin)
Gold, silver, and other minerals extracted from the earth by human effort
Minerals Rate
2.5% — if extracted amount reaches nisab (Hanbali/Hanafi view)
Hawl
No hawl — Zakat is due at extraction (like agricultural crops)
Basis
Hadith: "In rikāz, one-fifth is due." (Bukhari & Muslim)
05

Nisab & Hawl Explained

Nisab (نِصَاب) — The Minimum Threshold
Nisab is the minimum amount of wealth a Muslim must possess before Zakat becomes obligatory. It was set by the Prophet ﷺ to ensure only those genuinely above subsistence level are required to pay. It serves as the Islamic "tax-free threshold."
Hawl (حَوْل) — The Lunar Year
Hawl is the requirement that zakatable wealth remains at or above nisab for one complete lunar year (approximately 354 days). The clock resets if wealth drops below nisab at any point. Exception: agricultural produce and rikāz have no hawl requirement.

Recipients of Zakat — The 8 Asnaf

إِنَّمَا الصَّدَقَاتُ لِلْفُقَرَاءِ وَالْمَسَاكِينِ وَالْعَامِلِينَ عَلَيْهَا وَالْمُؤَلَّفَةِ قُلُوبُهُمْ وَفِي الرِّقَابِ وَالْغَارِمِينَ وَفِي سَبِيلِ اللَّهِ وَابْنِ السَّبِيلِ
Zakah expenditures are only for the poor and for the needy and for those employed to collect it and for bringing hearts together for Islam and for freeing captives or slaves and for those in debt and for the cause of Allah and for the stranded traveler — an obligation imposed by Allah.
Surah At-Tawbah 9:60
Visual Overview — The 8 Asnaf of Zakat

Arabic Term English Name Description
1 الْفُقَرَاء — Al-Fuqarā' The Poor Those who possess less than the nisab and cannot meet their basic needs. Their poverty is severe — they cannot find sufficient means for even half a year.
2 الْمَسَاكِين — Al-Masākīn The Needy Those slightly better off than the fuqarā' — they have some income or possessions but not enough to meet all their needs. Can find half their needs but not more.
3 الْعَامِلِين — Al-ʿĀmilīn Zakat Administrators (Āmil) Those officially appointed to collect, manage, and distribute Zakat. They receive a fair wage from the Zakat fund for their administrative services, regardless of their personal wealth.
4 الْمُؤَلَّفَة — Al-Muallafah Those Whose Hearts Are to Be Reconciled (Muallaf) New Muslims whose faith needs strengthening, or non-Muslims whose goodwill towards Islam is sought for the benefit of the community. Given Zakat to support their transition or goodwill.
5 الرِّقَاب — Al-Riqāb Those in Bondage / Slavery Historically, to free enslaved people. Contemporary application includes assisting people trapped in oppressive conditions, human trafficking victims, or those in inescapable debt bondage.
6 الْغَارِمِين — Al-Ghārimīn Those Overwhelmed by Debt Individuals who have incurred debts to meet lawful basic needs and are unable to repay them. Excludes debts incurred for sinful or wasteful purposes. Debt must have reached its due date.
7 فِي سَبِيلِ اللَّه — Fī Sabīl Allāh In the Cause of Allah Classically, those fighting in jihad (defensive warfare). Contemporary scholars extend this to include students of Islamic knowledge, dawah workers, and those engaged in building Islamic institutions.
8 ابْن السَّبِيل — Ibn al-Sabīl The Stranded Traveller / Wayfarer A traveller who is cut off from their resources, even if wealthy at home. Receives enough Zakat to return home or complete their lawful journey. Does not apply to those who left home knowing they had no funds.
Important Note on Distribution
Scholars disagree on whether Zakat must be distributed to all eight categories equally. The Shāfiʿī school requires it. The Ḥanafī school permits giving to any one category. The Mālikī and Ḥanbalī schools advise considering local need, prioritising the most deserving. All agree the decision is guided by public interest maṣlaḥah.
08

Importance & Wisdom of Zakat

🕌
Spiritual Purification
Zakat purifies the soul from greed and attachment to material wealth. It is an act of worship (ibādah) that strengthens one's relationship with Allah. The Quran describes it as a means of "purifying" both the wealth and the person.
⚖️
Social Justice & Equity
Zakat redistributes wealth from the affluent to the vulnerable, reducing poverty and narrowing economic inequality. It is an institutionalised mechanism of social welfare built into Islamic law itself, not a voluntary act.
🤝
Community Solidarity
By caring for the poor, Zakat fosters empathy, compassion, and brotherhood (ukhuwwah) within the Muslim community. It ensures no member of the Ummah is left without basic needs being met.
📈
Economic Stimulus
Zakat prevents the hoarding of wealth by putting idle capital into circulation. Economists note that Zakat functions as a wealth tax that encourages investment, as hoarded wealth decreases every year by 2.5%.
🙏
Gratitude to Allah
Zakat is an acknowledgement that all wealth ultimately belongs to Allah, and the human is its steward (khalifah). Giving Zakat expresses gratitude for Allah's favour and trust in His provision (tawakkul).
🌱
Blessing in Wealth
The Prophet ﷺ taught that charity does not decrease wealth. Zakat invites divine barakah (blessing) — the remaining wealth grows through Allah's grace, contrary to what the human ego fears.
"Protect your wealth by giving Zakat; treat your sick by giving charity; and prepare for the waves of calamity by making supplication."
Al-Tabarāni (with a good chain) | Also: "Charity does not decrease wealth." — Sahih Muslim, No. 2588
